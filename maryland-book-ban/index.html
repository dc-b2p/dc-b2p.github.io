Draconian New Restrictions on Books in Maryland State Prisons

On May 24, 2018, The Washington Post published an article detailing draconian new restrictions on books in Maryland state prisons. A few highlights from the article:

"Inmates may no longer receive books shipped from online retailers or sent by friends and relatives - and instead must purchase reading materials from two prison-approved vendors, in some cases at a higher cost... Inmates can buy 10 books every three months... [Prisoners], their families and advocates who contacted The Post worry the selection is narrow and listed prices, plus shipping costs, are often higher than the tab for used books from online retailers."

A follow-up article was published on May 31, 2018, describing the ACLU's opposition to the new policy. It noted, among other things, that neither of the two prison-approved vendors sell the Harry Potter books in the paperback format Maryland prisons require.

The ACLU wrote a powerful letter to Stephen Moyer of the Department of Public Safety and Correctional Services that is well worth reading, especially the list on page 5 of popular and classic books that cannot be obtained in paperback format from either of the approved vendors.

How You Can Help

If you are a Maryland resident and you are disturbed by these drastic new restrictions on book access for Maryland state prisoners, there are several things you can do:

Contact Governor Larry Hogan

Contact the governor and tell him that you object to the new Department of Public Safety and Correctional Services (DPSCS) restrictions, found in DPSCS Institutional Bulletin #2018-02, denying prisoners access to donated books. Please keep your comments polite and brief. Potential talking points include:

  • Prisons are more peaceful when prisoners are busy reading.
  • Education while behind bars leads to reduced recidivism upon release.
  • The selection of books offered by the two approved vendors is very limited. For example, neither vendor offers paperback copies of any Harry Potter books, any books by Langston Hughes, or any books by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
  • Not all prisoners can afford to purchase new books, so it is essential that charities like DC Books to Prisons be able to send free books to prisoners.
  • Governor Hogan's official biography notes his past work establishing a commission that conducted "a top-to-bottom review of every single regulation in the state" in order to "reduce unnecessary and overly burdensome constraints on Maryland's citizens". Overturning the onerous new policy on books for Maryland's prisoners would be in line with his values.
  • Being allowed to possess only ten books at a time causes hardship to prisoners who wish to remain in possession of reference works while also obtaining new books. For example, if a prisoner owns and wishes to keep a dictionary, a thesaurus, a GED preparation book, two textbooks, an Alcoholics Anonymous book, and a Bible, they will already be in possession of seven books and will be considered in possession of contraband if they order four books.

The governor can be contacted by phone at 410-974-3901 or 1-800-811-8336, by e-mail at governor.maryland.gov/mail/default.asp or by paper mail addressed to:

The Honorable Larry Hogan
Governor of Maryland
100 State Circle
Annapolis, MD 21401

Raise this issue with political candidates

Contact other candidates who are running for governor and ask them where they stand on allowing prisoners to have better access to books. Make it clear that this issue matters to voters.

DC Books to Prisons will never endorse any candidate, but we may publish information we receive about where candidates stand on this issue. Please send us a copy of any messages you receive from any candidate, including the incumbent.

Sign the ACLU's petition

Online petitions aren't nearly as effective as phone calls or paper letters, but some people may still wish to sign the ACLU's petition.

Thank you for caring about Maryland prisoners' access to books! We will update this page as new information becomes available. (The last update was on June 2, 2018.)
